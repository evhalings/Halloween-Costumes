Individual feature correlations are moderate, but effort × creativity as a combined term has the strongest correlation with voting outcomes (r=0.43 weighted). This suggests voters subconsciously rewarded costumes where high creative ambition was backed up by real construction commitment — creative ideas that felt "doable" or "low-effort" didn't translate into votes the same way.

Multiple linear regression using all four features simultaneously to predict vote score. R² = 0.43 (weighted) — the four features together explain about 43% of variance in vote outcomes. The remaining ~57% reflects personal taste, familiarity with the source material, and group dynamics not captured here. Overall opinion has by far the largest positive coefficient, followed by effort. The negative coefficients for creativity and recognizability in isolation are a multicollinearity artifact — when overall opinion is already in the model, the other terms adjust accordingly.

Voters were asked to rank what they value in a Halloween costume. Their stated preferences put Creativity #1 and Effort/Difficulty #5 (last). But the data tells a different story: effort × creativity together was the strongest predictor of vote outcomes — and effort alone outperformed recognizability in driving votes, despite recognizability being ranked #3 by voters. This is a classic stated vs revealed preference gap: people said they don't care about effort, but they voted for high-effort costumes.

Stated importance vs actual correlation with votes

Voters ranked features 1-5 by importance (lower mean = rated more important). Bars show actual Pearson r with weighted IRV score. The gap between stated and revealed is stark for effort.

The effort paradox

Effort was ranked last in stated importance (mean rank 4.19 out of 5), yet it has the second-highest actual correlation with vote outcomes — and the strongest interaction with creativity.
